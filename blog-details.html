Fade Curves: The difference between “smooth” and “alive”

A practical lighting note for theatre cues and film transitions.

Read time: 6 minutes

A fade is not decoration. A fade is the timing of belief: when the audience decides a change is happening, when they accept it, and when they forget it ever changed.

1) The cue has a purpose

If the cue is a reveal, the rise must feel intentional. If it’s a memory, the change must feel like it came from inside the scene—not from the console.

2) The audience tracks contrast, not intensity

Percentage values are meaningless without context. The eye follows edges: what gets protected, what gets removed, and what stays still.

3) Write fades like dialogue

A “smooth” fade is often just a long one. An “alive” fade respects the actor’s timing and the director’s breath. Start with the beat. Then decide whether the change should be noticed.
